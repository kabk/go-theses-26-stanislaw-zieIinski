'Old style’ numerals, also called text figures, are usually referred to as more pleasant to read. This feeling is somehow caused by them blending more into the pattern created by lowercase letters. Shapes are slightly aletered and the digits are shifted on the vertical axis. Therefore the old style version of the numeral “9” is much closer to the lowercase ‘q’ or a single-storey ‘g’ than its lining substitute. 
	Out of nine web-safe typefaces, mentioned on Web3, only one – Georgia–uses old-style figures by default. In fact the common version of the font does not include lining numerals at all. The Sholes & Glidden Type Writer from 1874 only typed capital letters. The top row of its early-qwerty keyboard included digits from "2" to "9"— a decision similar to the one in Treviso Arithmetic was made, "I" and "O" keys typed both: digits and letters, producing the same movement for the machine but a different meaning for the human operator.
	As text entry interfaces continued to develop, lowercase letters, zero and eventually the digit 1 (*Foonote: )found their place on standardised keyboards. Different movement for the machine, but a confusion for the human operator. "Wired to an undernet of imperceptible connections and lines, she decrypts and encodes, switching and swapping in the exchange." Whether it had to do with letter shapes constrained by manufacturing possibilities, or later, the small screen resolutions, the visual proximity of '0' and 'O' started to complicate the exchange of information.(Ref:D.Wendt) Call signs,  part numbers, storage print-outs, reference numbers, registration plates made perfect use of the 'extended alphabet'; but without context to support the reader, '1' could be mistaken for lowercase 'l' more often.

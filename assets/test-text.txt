A default set of numerals, arranged in the order they appear on most phone and computer keyboards in widespread use today. I know this set as lining numerals—a term that belongs to a naming scheme shared by design software and Unicode conventions. The name describes a specific behaviour: lining numerals sit on the baseline and share uniform height, which in most typefaces is set to match, or come very close to, the height of capital letters. 
If a font contains the necessary glyphs, software settings make it possible to display a slightly different set of numerals. (Footnote: I usually this options in a design applications, e.g.: InDesign; and in web development by describing the font properties in a stylesheet language document.)

'Oldstyle' numerals — also called text figures — are generally referred to as more comfortable to read within a running text. This feeling is usually attributed to the way they fit into the pattern created by lowercase letters. Unlike lining numerals they vary in height: some have ascenders that rise above the x-height, some have descenders that drop below the baseline, others sit within the x-height bounds. This way the old style version of the numeral “9” is much closer to the lowercase ‘q’ or a single-storey ‘g’ than its lining substitute. 
Out of the nine web-safe typefaces, only one — Georgia — uses oldstyle figures by default.(Footnote: Web safe fonts according to W3 schools: https://www.w3schools.com/cssref/css_websafe_fonts.php) In fact, the standard version of the font does not include lining numerals at all. Georgia was designed by Matthew Carter for Microsoft as a sturdy, dependable typeface for online reading on low-resolutions of screens at a time – the first version was released in 1996. Today Microsoft's website points to its numerals as one of the most important features, describing them as 'slightly non-aligning', which they say is 'a characteristic that imparts a flavor of individuality to any page set in Georgia.' Description: Set of figures from the typeface "Bell" by Richard Austin for Joseph Bell. 

"Bell," a typeface cut in 1788 by the British punchcutter Richard Austin for the British Letter Foundry, is often mentioned as the one that popularised the lining numerals and influenced the later Scotch Roman faces mentioned by Carter. Each number in 'Bell' is the same height, approximately three-quarters of the capital letters—a rarely seen feature in typefaces today. Yet, in his book Letters of Credit (1986), Walter Tracy, a British type designer, writes that the numeral forms of 'Bell' "blend well with the lowercase alphabet," and traces their uniform heights back to large letterforms from the Caslon foundry in the 1780s. The reason for the transformation itself is believed to be an answer to the growing need for shop signs and placards on the British streets in the 18th century, where numerals informed the public about the prices. Digital reproductions of various type specimens from the 1780s seem to support that theory, as the early examples of lining numerals were indeed presented mostly in larger type sizes with a currency symbol next to them. In those specimens the smaller sizes, naturally still use the oldstyle figures; to me, the three-quarters-height numerals from "Bell," are a compromise between the loud window-display lure for customers and a more gentle image of a digit for the readers.

A similar mention of Bell can be found in one of the most influential books on typography—The Elements of Typographic Style by a Canadian typographer Robert Bringhurst. It is a collection of typographic principles discussed in an open way tracing various tendencies in the field. In a section dedicated to numerals, capitals and small caps, speculating on the origin of lining figures, he points towards the 19th century typographers who 'stretched these [Bell's] figures up to cap height' thus making them a norm in commercial typography. He names the later visible lack of oldstyle figures in fonts like Frutiger's Méridien or Zapf's Comenius calling it a possible 'commercial censorship' calling for a retroactive supply of oldstyle numbers to typefaces that have been issued without.

That journey from the ranging to lining figures makes me wonder why the Hindu-Arabic numeral forms have not developed in similar way as the Roman alphabet—perhaps changing from lining to oldstyle would be as easy and common as changing from lowercase to uppercase and wouldn't be reserved for typographers.

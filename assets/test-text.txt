'Old style’ numerals, also called text figures, are usually referred to as more pleasant to read. This feeling is somehow caused by them blending more into the pattern created by lowercase letters. Shapes are slightly aletered and the digits are shifted on the vertical axis. Therefore the old style version of the numeral “9” is much closer to the lowercase ‘q’ or a single-storey ‘g’ than its lining substitute. 
	Out of nine web-safe typefaces, mentioned on Web3, only one – Georgia–uses old-style figures by default. In fact the common version of the font does not include lining numerals at all. (*Footnote: there is also a Georgia Pro family available for purchase from Carter&Cone–this one uses lining numerals following the standard convention of retail typefaces) . It was designed by Matthew Carter, for Microsoft, as a companion to the sans-serif Verdana. The main goal of the design was to create a 'sturdy, dependable serif typeface for optimal clarity in online reading'(*from Carter&Cone site). The first version was released in 1996 by Microsoft.

Set of numerals from Bell typeface as it is presented in The Elements of Typographic Style. Robert Bringhurst suggests that the proportions introduced by Richard Austin in 1788 were punchcutters’ interpretations of numbers handwritten by shopkeepers on placards and shop signs. They are approximately two-thirds of the cap-height. Caught in a different context, they are a street-level decoy slowly growing to match the height of capital letters.

The figure above is a set of numerals presented as an “indian wonder” in Codex Vigilanus, a compilation of historical documents, assembled in what is now southern Spain around 10th century. It is often mentioned as the first work that introduced the hindu-arabic numerals to Europe. The first time I saw this image online directed me to G. Ifrah’s “The universal history of numbers”—an attempt to present the origins of the world counting systems. 
“the full and complete answer to all and any questions that anyone might want to ask about the history of numbers and of counting, from prehistory to the age of computers.” 
Even though I was not looking for a ‘complete answer’, the book interests me for it’s variety of graphical symbols, and simplified visual representations of the concepts it talks about. A few negative reviews online, one that classifies Ifrah’s work as a ‘deception’. I continued to browse through the online copy. It’s an intense, 600+ pages book in a square format, so unpleasent to look at on my horizontal screen.  

One sub-chapter I found particularly relevant to that way of thinking, and even funny is titled “Fanciful explanations for the origin of “Arabic” numerals” p.356  A set of six, unlikely, hypotheses from various times and places, each of them speculates the origins of numerical forms seeking their beginnings in some closed systems like mapping the strokes onto the sides and diagonals of a square etc. Ifrah criticises them for being ‘sterile’, therefore far away from the truth, and sends the reader to his own, quite complex, diagrams that aim to simulate possible evolutions and journeys as they travlled the world across centuries.

A few hours later I’ve found a better reproduction of Treviso’s Arithmetic through British Library, Incunabula Short Title Catalogue (ISTC). It was scanned by someone and uploaded online for public access via BEIC  Milan Library online viewer. Despite the poor quality of the electronic facsimile it seems clear that the characters for lowercase “i” and “o” were used to represent one and zero accordingly. Punchuctters’ and/or printers’ decisions to fuse those symbols were probably directed by time efficiency.  Forging additional characters would require extra work which would influence the economy of production. I can’t be sure if the financial gain was the actual reason behind those decisions, but even looking at the online copy, unaware of the writing conventions from that time, the context in which the characters appear on the page is enough to tell their functions apart.

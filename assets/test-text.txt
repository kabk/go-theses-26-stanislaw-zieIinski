'Old style’ numerals, also called text figures, are usually referred to as more pleasant to read. This feeling is somehow caused by them blending more into the pattern created by lowercase letters. Shapes are slightly aletered and the digits are shifted on the vertical axis. Therefore the old style version of the numeral “9” is much closer to the lowercase ‘q’ or a single-storey ‘g’ than its lining substitute. 
	Out of nine web-safe typefaces, mentioned on Web3, only one – Georgia–uses old-style figures by default. In fact the common version of the font does not include lining numerals at all. (*Footnote: there is also a Georgia Pro family available for purchase from Carter&Cone–this one uses lining numerals following the standard convention of retail typefaces) . It was designed by Matthew Carter, for Microsoft, as a companion to the sans-serif Verdana. The main goal of the design was to create a 'sturdy, dependable serif typeface for optimal clarity in online reading'(*from Carter&Cone site). The first version was released in 1996 by Microsoft.

Set of figures from the typeface "Bell" as it is presented in The Elements of Typographic Style. Robert Bringhurst suggests that the proportions introduced by Richard Austin in 1788 were punchcutters’ interpretations of numbers handwritten by shopkeepers on placards and shop signs. They are approximately two-thirds of the cap-height. Caught in a different context, they are a street-level decoy, slowly growing to match the height of capital letters. Was it an attempt to separate them from the alphabet and give them their own case?

Ifrah’s “The universal history of numbers”—an attempt to present the origins of the world counting systems. In the foreword it says: “(...)the full and complete answer to all and any questions that anyone might want to ask about the history of numbers and of counting, from prehistory to the age of computers.” (footnote: Ifrah, Numbers.. no page number)
some parts managed to escape the moot intent of providing a ‘complete answer’ . The book interests me for it’s variety of graphical symbols that aim to compress and compare the complex systems used to signify counting across history. The illustrations themselves could be used as a visual history of the development of mathematical thought, they are ordered, structured and analysed perhaps aiming at'universality' but strictly bound to conventions of an encyclopaedic book like that. It also has a few negative reviews online, one that classifies Ifrah’s work as a ‘deception’. I continued to browse through the online copy.  It is an intense document, 600+ pages in a square format.

One sub-chapter I found close to the way of thinking that seeks complete asnwers. “Fanciful explanations for the origin of “Arabic” numerals” p.356  A set of six, 'unlikely', hypotheses coined mostly by european monks and histotians, each of them speculates the origins of numerical forms trying to find a script or recipe for the way they were made. Looking for relations between form and meaning they try to reverse engineer them. Ifrah, quite reasonably, criticises those speculations for being ‘sterile’, therefore far away from the truth. Later in the book he provides his own, quite complex, diagrams that simulate possible evolutions and journeys of figure graphemes as they were borrowed, reinterpreted and re-mixed.

 I’ve found a better reproduction of Treviso’s Arithmetic through British Library's, Incunabula Short Title Catalogue (ISTC). It is available for public access via BEIC Milan Library online viewer. It is an early example of european press work.

At that time hindu-arabic numerals were already known in Europe for a few centuries. The growth of printing press, and the following standardisation influenced the shapes of the digits, early calendars show that quite well. Treviso Arithmetic however seems to use the characters for lowercase “i” and “o” to represent one and zero accordingly. Punchuctters’ and/or printers’ decisions to fuse them could have been dictated by the ever-present longing for efficiency. Forging additional characters would require extra work which would influence the economy of production. There was a trust put in the reader. I can’t be sure if the financial gain, or time savings were the deciding factors behind those choices, maybe they were already close enough and to make similar shapes was thought of as simply unnecessary, even looking at the poor quality online copy, unaware of the writing conventions from that period, the context in which those characters appear on the page is enough to tell their functions apart.

The Sholes & Glidden Type Writer from 1874 only typed capital letters. It had a qwerty keyboard — a result of optimisation for telegraphic communication and typing convenience. The top row included digits from "2" to "9", a similar decision to that of printers' of Treviso Arithmetic was made, I and O typed both digits and letters, producing the same mechanical motion but a different meaning for the human readers.
